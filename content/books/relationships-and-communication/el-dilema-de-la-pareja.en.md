{{< lead >}}
A deep, judgment-free analysis of infidelity: why it happens, what it means, and what a couple can do after it.
{{< /lead >}}

### Topic
Infidelity and what it reveals about modern relationships.

### Approach
Relational psychotherapy grounded in real clinical cases; examines infidelity from both sides —the one who was betrayed and the one who strayed— seeking to understand before judging.

### Why Read It
* Challenges the simplistic "victim and villain" framing of infidelity.
* Explores why people in happy relationships also cheat.
* Provides a language to talk about exclusivity, desire, and trust before (or after) a crisis.

### Common Objections
- **“It justifies infidelity”**: it doesn’t; it examines it in order to understand it and decide with clarity.
- **“It’s uncomfortable to read”**: on purpose; it questions assumptions couples almost never talk about.

### For Whom
People who have lived through infidelity —on either side— and want to understand what happened, or couples who prefer to discuss exclusivity before it becomes a crisis.

### Not For
Those looking for simple moral condemnation or a foolproof formula to “affair-proof” their relationship.

---

### Key Ideas
- Infidelity also happens in happy relationships; it’s not always a symptom of a broken couple.
- It’s often less about sex and more about a longing for aliveness, novelty, or reconnecting with a lost version of oneself.
- After an affair, a relationship can end, survive, or transform into a new and more honest one.
- Trust and exclusivity are not to be assumed: they are discussed and redefined.

### How to Apply
- Talk with your partner about what fidelity means to each of you, without assuming you share the same definition.
- If there was an affair, explore its meaning first (what was being sought, what was missing) before deciding the relationship's future.
- Redefine agreements and boundaries explicitly at each stage of the relationship.
